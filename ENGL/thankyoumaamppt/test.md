# Tone

## Definition

The tone of a literary work: perspective or attitude that the author adopts with regards to a specific character, place or development.

Tone can portray a variety of emotions ranging from solemn, grave, and critical to witty, wry and humorous. Tone helps the reader ascertain the writer's feelings towards a particular topic and this in turn influences the reader's understanding of the story.

## Example

> The large woman simply turned around and **kicked** him right square in his blue-jeaned sitter. Then she reached down, picked the boy up by his shirt front, and shook him until his teeth **rattled**.



In the story "Thank You Ma'am," the tone serves as a critical element that shapes the reader's understanding and emotional response to the narrative. The tone can be described as a mix of sternness, compassion, and ultimately, redemption. Here's an analysis of the tone in different parts of the story with examples:

1. **Sternness**: The tone starts off stern and authoritative, particularly when Mrs. Luella Bates Washington Jones confronts the boy who tried to snatch her purse. The language used is direct and commanding, reflecting her disapproval and anger at the boy's actions. For example, when she kicks the boy and shakes him, the narrative conveys a stern tone: "The large woman simply turned around and kicked him right square in his blue-jeaned sitter. Then she reached down, picked the boy up by his shirt front, and shook him until his teeth rattled."

2. **Compassion**: As the story progresses, the tone shifts to one of compassion and understanding. This is evident when Mrs. Jones decides to take the boy home instead of turning him over to the police. Her actions and words show a softer side, indicating that she sees the boy not as a criminal but as someone in need of guidance. For instance, when she says, "You ought to be my son. I would teach you right from wrong," the tone conveys a sense of maternal concern and a desire to help the boy.

3. **Humility and Shared Humanity**: There's a subtle tone of humility and shared humanity when Mrs. Jones admits to having done things she's not proud of, creating a connection between her and the boy. This shared understanding humanizes both characters and adds depth to the narrative. The tone is reflected in her statement: "I have done things, too, which I would not tell you, son — neither tell God, if he didn’t already know."

4. **Redemption**: Towards the end of the story, the tone becomes one of redemption and hope. Mrs. Jones gives the boy money for shoes and advice for the future, indicating a belief in his capacity to change. The tone is hopeful and encouraging, as seen when she says, "Take this ten dollars and buy yourself some blue suede shoes. And next time, do not make the mistake of latching onto my pocketbook nor nobody else’s."

5. **Respect and Gratitude**: The final tone of the story is one of respect and gratitude. The boy, though initially unable to express himself fully, manages to say "Thank you" before Mrs. Jones shuts the door. This simple expression conveys a deep sense of gratitude and respect for the kindness and understanding shown to him by Mrs. Jones.

In summary, the tone in "Thank You Ma'am" varies from stern to compassionate, humble, redemptive, and finally respectful, reflecting the transformation of both the characters and the reader's perception of the situation. These shifts in tone are crucial in conveying the story's message about the potential for change and the power of human connection.
